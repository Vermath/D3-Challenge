What health risks are particular demographics faced with across the United States?

Poverty and the Uninsured:

Adults in low-income households are more likely to be uninsured than people in higher income households, but not all uninsured adults are in families with low incomes.

Poverty and Smoking:

In the U.S., people living below the poverty line have higher rates of cigarette smoking than the general population.

Poverty and Obesity:

The U.S. is one of the wealthiest countries in the world and accordingly has high obesity rates.

Age and the Uninsured:

Compared with other age groups, young adults are the most likely to go without coverage.

Age and Smoking:

It does appear that states with a large proportion of older age groups tend to have the highest rates of smoking.

Age and Obesity:

There does appear to be slight correlation between a states median age and level of obesity. Clearly there are other factors, like poverty and income that may affect obesity levels.

Income and the Uninsured:

Many uninsured people cite the high cost of insurance as the main reason they lack coverage.

Income and Smoking:

A household's income contributes directly to the level of poverty. Poverty is directly related to likelihood to smoke. Ergo, poverty and smoking are correlated.

Income and Obesity:

Overall obesity prevalence does tend to decrease with increased levels of income.
